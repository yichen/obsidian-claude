# **My Feedback for the Spark-on-K8s RFC**

Author: [Yi Chen](mailto:yichen@stripe.com)    Created: Jan 16, 2025  

The [Spark-on-Kubernetes RFC](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0) shared in February 2024 lacked solid engineering rationale and supporting details.  The [executive summary](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.nh2n0nbeuxvo) section explained the main goal and benefit of the project: improved reliability, user experience, performance, and potentially significant cost savings. In my opinion, we are in a very good place for all of these dimensions on EMR, and I worry that the current spark-on-k8s execution appears to carry the risk of increased cost and reduced reliability (spot instances) and user experience (browsable logs).

The content from the RFC is quoted with yellow highlights for easier understanding.

# Feedback for the Challenges Section

* **Not multi-tenant**. See my [comment](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?disco=AAABEcBjPvQ) thread from Feb.  
* **Managing excessive amounts Infra and Chef recipes**   
  * YARN stack has a smaller number of infra components compared to Kubernetes stack, with smaller surface area, and more publicly accessible information for learning, development and debugging (through google search).  
  * Chef and Terraform are well-known technologies. There is significant technical debt in our implementation. This is a technical debt that can be fixed with much less engineering effort and risk compared to moving to Kubernetes.   
  * Airbnb, the largest EMR customer in the world, does not experience this issue (regarding Chef and Terraform) similar to our S3 permission. Intuitively it means our Chef/Terraform challenges are not related to EMR, but our own implementation.   
* **Limited auto scaling capability**  
  * This is based on the wrong assumption that we should depend on spot instances and better autoscaling. See my [comments](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?disco=AAABEcBjP4A) in Feb.   
  * Our [execution in 2024](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1az7FL1n3xcrkl95PMSFzidyag8uRdWOWdQ8p38OKcSQ/edit?tab=t.0#heading=h.lnqfcyy21mit) has proven that not only we should avoid using spot instances, we should reduce autoscaling instead of the proposed faster autoscaling.  
  * Karpenter is faster for autoscaling, by only 30 seconds according to AWS techtalk \[TODO: add the link\]. Its benefit compared to Kubernetes AutoScaler is mainly reducing the delay from the k8s layer to the cloud provider layer, which is about 30s to 1 minute delay.  
* **EMR is AZ specific**  
  * This is based on inaccurate assumptions. Pre-2024 EMR infrastructure mainly depends on spot instances, and multi-AZ provides better instance availability. Spot instances have no price advantage compared to ODCR with Savings Plan, especially for clusters with minimum autoscaling needs (e.g. prod-data-platform-emr6).  
  * The Spark-on-EKS roadmap for FY26, which includes adopting spot instances and multi-AZ autoscaling, will likely achieve the opposite of its intended goals. It will lead to resource fragmentation since the **us-east-1** region has **six Availability Zones (AZs)**, effectively creating six physical clusters, each scaling independently. This fragmentation makes it challenging to purchase AZ-specific ODCRs, as each zone would need to be accounted for separately with unpredictable scaling patterns.  
* **EMR clusters lack observability**  
  * Even though AWS EMR is a “managed” service, it requires a lot of customization and many times it doesn’t behave as it should and acts like a black box.  It is based on the incorrect assumption that, as a managed service, EMR should provide everything fully configured and ready to use. However, Spark configuration has always been the responsibility of the user, whether it’s the infrastructure team or the end user, regardless of where Spark runs. The fact that the Spark-on-Kubernetes   
  * Managing Spark workloads on AWS EMR can be intricate due to the involvement of various systems like Livy, Yarn, Spark, and HDFS. This surface area is significantly smaller than the surface area of the Spark-on-k8s project, especially considering new technologies with a very small user base and little publicly accessible knowledge. Among these stacks, Livy is the only one that is not mature and the only one that has given us trouble. Most of the key dependencies for Spark-on-Kubernetes, are in the same state of Livy, including YuniKorn, Celeborn, Karpenter (for Spark), with little publicly google-able content.   
  * Not every component in these systems offers clear observability, sometimes making EMR clusters seem like a black box. I don’t know how the container-based observability can benefit DI team and Spark users during investigation and root-causing. It is also not clear what does it mean by EMR to seem like a blackbox. It is just a YARN provider like all its competitors (e.g. Cloudera). I think we may actually face degraded observability due to limited log access from the Spark History Server.  
  * This leads to a lot of tickets with AWS Support and doing RCA is very difficult.  In my opinion, the high volume of AWS support tickets was not due to observability tooling gaps or fundamental issues with AWS/EMR. It primarily stemmed from a lack of knowledge and experience within the team. None of the issues I investigated in 2024 were attributable to AWS or EMR problems (but I am aware other team members may have seen issues related to EMR, e.g. Iceberg). There is also no evidence on how the claimed better observability on Kubernetes would help. My personal experience is that not only moving to Kubernetes will not help, it will be more difficult to root-cause issues with no direct log access from Spark History Server.   
  * Lack of searchable logs. Searchable logs are beneficial when the users know what to search for. It is especially helpful for root cause analysis, but primarily when there is already a strong intuition about the issue and a clear idea of what to search for. YARN-based logs, however, offer a much better user experience for developing that initial intuition and identifying early clues. I anticipate that time-to-first-clue during future Spark debugging will be significantly longer due to the lack of direct log access from the Spark History Server and the inability to visually scan log files along a natural timeline. Today, some advanced users can read Spark driver logs because it is browsable, showing a clear error stack that can easily be identifiable visually. I am concerned that most of these advanced users will give up reading Spark driver logs   
* **EMR doesn’t support Spark client mode**  
  * This is simply not true. See my [comments](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?disco=AAABEcBjP4Y) in Feb.  
  * Airbnb is the largest EMR customer in the world, and it has been running Spark with client mode since 2018\.

# Feedback for the Benefits Section

* It’s unclear how “[kubernets](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.w01kr25bb9y)”, “[multi-tenancy](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.w01kr25bb9y)”, “[multi-AZ deploys](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.a6oxhq11d07n)” are considered benefits. None of these benefits our Spark users (e.g. DIG or MF teams) or Slack (in terms of cost). Our stakeholders mostly care about cost (Slack), reliability, on-timeness, and user experience.  
* [Autoscaling](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.a6oxhq11d07n)  I think our users don’t care about autoscaling, they mostly care about reliability, on-time, and user experience. Autoscaling comes with risks for reliability and cost, because whenever autoscaling is happening, either scaling up or scaling down, the resource utilization ratio is very low. The RFC’s claim that Karpenter’s faster autoscaling is an advantage is misguided. According to AWS, Karpenter is only about **30 seconds faster** (mainly by reducing the overhead and combining a traditional two-step autoscaling into one-step). Additionally, faster scaling can lead to significant cost increases, as each autoscaling action may result in lower resource utilization. Autoscaling will further expose our infrastructure to InsufficientInstanceCapacityError, which is trending up. It has impacted the Metric Foundation team with its workload in the DMA cluster in late 2024 and will become more urgent in 2025\.  
* [Improved observability](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.eoekuf7k4eo5). The RFC claims that these new observability features can help end users tune their jobs, but I am unclear on how they would be beneficial and would need further clarification. It appears that we may be in a worse situation for observability due to the loss of direct log access from the Spark History Server.   
* [Supported by AWS EMR](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.1pnl98nk6ehb). Our execution did not align with the approach suggested in the RFC. Instead of using the **EMR-on-EKS** option proposed, we are now pursuing one of the directions listed under **“rejected alternatives”**—open-source Spark on Kubernetes—despite the 2022 AWS benchmark highlighting significant cost and performance penalties with this approach.  
* [Developer velocity](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.numazbuomvqq). I agree that this can benefit the DI team, but [not a benefit for Spark users in Slack](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?disco=AAAA82icGHw). We can also run any open-source Spark version on EMR as long as it's compatible with the YARN version. I am concerned that learning a totally new platform would set back the velocity for a long time for both the DI team and our users.   
* [Searchable Spark Logs](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.899zh32zc06u). Searchable logs are only useful for users who know what to search for. Users typically develop this understanding after gaining some initial intuition from clues, which are often obtained through browsable logs. Currently, we lack direct log access from the Spark History Server. This change would not benefit our users and the DI team.   
* [Cost Efficiency](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.orxl9cr0i4dt). The current project direction (using open-source Spark on Kubernetes) contradicts with this goal, because  [AWS’s 2022 benchmark](https://slack-pde.slack.com/archives/CJFHE7AR3/p1736281264536029?thread_ts=1733506214.065929&cid=CJFHE7AR3), which shows that open-source Spark on EKS can cost up to **250%** that of the cost on EMR, with significant performance penalty.  
  * There is no cost advantage to using spot instances compared to on-demand instances covered by Savings Plans (see [Spot Or Not](https://docs.google.com/document/u/0/d/1J9AwR2G_UM10IvGB2bZp_TefA6DZU_D2rgCvtgUi0UM/edit)).   
  * “Kubernetes stands as the reigning container orchestration system. Its prowess in optimizing resource allocation”  This appears to be irrelevant. It seems to me that the Spark-on-Kubernetes ecosystem has largely stalled since 2022\.  
  * [Stitchfix](https://multithreaded.stitchfix.com/blog/2022/03/14/spark-eks/) reported a 45-55% infrastructure cost reduction for all Spark-based computations after transitioning from EMR on EC2 to EMR on EKS.    
    * A closer look at the content reveals that the vast majority of their workload consists of ad-hoc, interactive jobs (e.g., from notebooks). Such workloads typically lack strict SLA requirements, have a high tolerance for failure, and exhibit unpredictable, unsustainable usage patterns, making them a better fit for more flexible autoscaling solutions.  
    * At Slack, similar workloads are handled in our staging cluster, so migrating those to EKS would be reasonable. However, there are also a good number of workloads with strict SLA requirements, including those run by the DIG and MF teams.   
  * [Enabling Spark client mode](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.2ryb7wpnb5jq). EMR does support Spark client mode, making this benefit invalid. Airbnb, the largest EMR customer, has been using Spark in client mode since 2018\.   
  * [Recruitment Benefits](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.19pao3487so2).   
    * **Spark-on-Kubernetes** as a technology has lost momentum since 2022 and become a niche technology, which could limit our hiring pool due to its specialized nature. It is much more important for the DI team to hire for infrastructure knowledge (e.g. Spark, Trino), instead of generic Kubernetes DevOps skills. It is much easier for an engineer with infrastructure knowledge to learn generic Kubernetes DevOps skill, than the other way around.   
    * Our existing engineers will learn a great deal by supporting **YuniKorn**, **Karpenter (for Spark)**, and **Celeborn,** all of which have a very small user community**.** These are not marketable skills until the whole ecosystem reverses the trend (YuniKorn/Spark-on-k8s lost momentum since 2022\)  
  * [Other companies are using Spark on k8s](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16b33Vrt0v_5rufwMgtrYkaxJsbxjrLP8c8enXoTDRig/edit?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.smr2x46x039w). It is challenging to find peer companies that have adopted Spark on Kubernetes, which I currently view as a risk rather than a benefit. The ecosystem appears to have lost momentum in 2022, though this may change in the future.  
    * **Apple** is a large company that has adopted Spark-on-Kubernetes, likely due to its unique security requirements. Apple also has a large team of experts, including 5 PMC for Spark, and 5 PMC for YuniKorn (with only one overlapping PMC). We can’t compare our DI team with Apple’s Slack team.  
    * **Pinterest** is an early adopter of Spark-on-Kubernetes, although it is too early to tell if this is a success story or a cautionary tale.  
      * As of Pinterest’s blog published on October **23, 2024**, Pinterest had been migrating its Spark workloads to **Spark-on-Kubernetes** for a year and a half and had only migrated 50% of its workload. Pinterest has started investing in Spark-on-Kubernetes as early as [**2020**](https://medium.com/pinterest-engineering/empowering-pinterest-data-scientists-and-machine-learning-engineers-with-pyspark-f41b0d1dd1b8), long before Slack.  
      * Notably, they faced substantial difficulties with **YuniKorn** and had to [contribute directly](https://medium.com/pinterest-engineering/resource-management-with-apache-yunikorn-for-apache-spark-on-aws-eks-at-pinterest-0dba3afb4609) to its development to unblock their progress, highlighting the immaturity of this key dependency.  
      * Also note that **Pinterest** is migrating from **Spark-on-EC2** to **Spark-on-Kubernetes**, whereas **Slack** is migrating from **Spark-on-EMR** to **Spark-on-Kubernetes**. The ROI equation is significantly different between these two paths. As a comparison, Airbib migrated from Spark-on-EC2 to Spark-on-EMR to significantly reduce operational costs, and Stripe/LinkedIn/Uber are all still running Spark-on-EC2.  
    * It appears that **Lyft** uses Spark-on-Kubernetes for its machine learning infrastructure called [LyftLearn](https://eng.lyft.com/lyftlearn-ml-model-training-infrastructure-built-on-kubernetes-aef8218842bb). Machine learning (ML) use cases are one of the good niches where Spark-on-Kubernetes is a good fit. It addresses [dependency challenges due to the strong reproducibility requirements for ML jobs](https://slack-pde.slack.com/archives/CJFHE7AR3/p1722446301870589?thread_ts=1722443355.342529&cid=CJFHE7AR3) (isolation of C/C++ libraries). Additionally, ML workloads tend to be iterative, unlike typical ETL workloads.   
    * Both **Databricks** and **Cloudera** contribute to key Spark-on-Kubernetes technologies. However, they are platform providers, similar to **AWS**, where the customers—not the platforms—are responsible for managing costs. I believe their interest in this investment is to compete with EMR-on-EKS. We should consider them as a provider, similar to AWS, instead of a peer proof.   
    * **Uber** undertook a two-year effort to containerize parts of their [critical Hadoop infrastructure](https://www.uber.com/blog/hadoop-namenode-container/), but their Spark workloads still run with **CapacityScheduler** in YARN (as of October 2022 when I met their whole Spark team). Uber has a significantly larger and more advanced data platform team than Slack. The fact that they containerized their **NameNode** but did not continue the effort to run **Spark on Kubernetes** serves as a cautionary tale rather than a validation of the approach.  
    * **Twitter** began migrating to Spark-on-Kubernetes but reversed direction halfway through after encountering significant technical challenges. Their decision to abandon the migration raises important concerns for Slack.
