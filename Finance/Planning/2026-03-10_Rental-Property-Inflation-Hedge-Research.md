## 3. Does Real Estate Actually Hedge Inflation?

### The Academic/Expert Consensus

| Source | Finding |
|--------|---------|
| CFA Institute (2025) | Real assets (real estate, commodities, infrastructure) are effective inflation diversifiers historically |
| ScienceDirect (2025) | New international analysis confirms housing hedges inflation — but with **significant regional variation** |
| Investopedia | REITs can hedge inflation but "mimic equities during volatile markets" |
| Plante Moran | Real estate hedges inflation over the **long term** (10+ year horizon) — short-term correlation is weak |
| Motley Fool (2026) | I Bonds yield 4.03% (0.90% fixed + 3.12% inflation) — a guaranteed inflation hedge with zero risk |

### The Nuances

**When real estate works as an inflation hedge:**
- You have a **fixed-rate mortgage** (your payment stays flat while rents/values inflate) — this is the primary mechanism
- You're in a **supply-constrained** market with strong demand drivers
- You hold for **10+ years** (short-term correlation with inflation is weak)
- Inflation is **demand-driven** (economy growing, wages rising)

**When it fails:**
- **Supply-driven/cost-push inflation** (tariffs, energy, supply chains) — which is the current environment
- **Rising mortgage rates** suppress buyer demand and property values
- **Stagflation** — high inflation + weak economy (see section 4)
- **Cash-flow-negative** property — you're paying to hold the hedge, unlike I Bonds or TIPS which pay you

## 4. Stagflation Scenarios (High Inflation + Recession)

### 1970s Historical Performance

| Asset Class | Real Annual Return (1970s) | Notes |
|-------------|---------------------------|-------|
| Real estate | **+4.5%** | Outperformed stocks and bonds |
| Stocks (S&P 500) | Negative real returns | Worst decade for equities |
| Bonds | Negative real returns | Inflation destroyed bond values |

**However, critical context:**
- Mortgage rates hit **18%+ by 1981** — devastating for transaction activity and affordability
- Regional variation was enormous — some areas boomed, others stagnated
- Real estate values rose *nominally* but the ability to *sell* was severely impaired by 18% mortgage rates
- Housing affordability crashed, reducing the buyer pool
- The 1970s housing "gains" came primarily from **leverage** on a fixed-rate mortgage (the exact mechanism you'd benefit from)

### Current Stagflation Risk (March 2026)

- Tariff pressures, supply chain disruptions, and energy volatility mirror 1970s conditions
- Most economists expect any stagflation to be **short-lived** rather than a prolonged 1970s repeat
- **Key difference from 1970s**: You already own with a presumably low fixed-rate mortgage, so rising rates hurt *buyers* (not you) — but that also means fewer buyers when you eventually sell

### Reddit/Investor Sentiment
- "In stagflation, real estate has actually tanked due to its close relationship with the economy" (r/Fire)
- Consensus: real estate is illiquid during stagflation — prices may hold nominally but transaction volume drops

## Summary Assessment

### Arguments FOR Holding (Inflation Hedge)

1. **Fixed-rate mortgage leverage**: Your mortgage payment stays at $1,780 regardless of inflation — this is the core inflation hedge mechanism
2. **Historical precedent**: Real estate returned +4.5% real during 1970s stagflation, outperforming stocks and bonds
3. **Tax deferral**: Selling triggers $123K in capital gains tax; holding defers this indefinitely (or until death for stepped-up basis)
4. **Irreplaceable asset**: Once sold at today's rates, you can't re-enter the market at a 3-4% mortgage
5. **Long-term appreciation**: Seattle/Eastside fundamentals (tech jobs, limited land) support long-term growth

### Arguments FOR Selling (Stronger, in Your Situation)

1. **Terrible cap rate (0.9–2.2%)**: Even I Bonds at 4.03% beat this with zero risk
2. **Cash-flow negative**: You're *paying* -$405/mo to hold this "hedge" — true inflation hedges pay you
3. **Opportunity cost is enormous**: $700K equity earning 0.9% vs. 8–10% in equities = $50K–63K/yr gap
4. **Eastside market is weakening**: -16% YoY prices, +49% inventory — selling conditions may worsen
5. **Rents are flat/declining on Eastside**: The "rents rise with inflation" thesis is NOT playing out in your submarket
6. **Your cash flow is critical**: At -$733/mo net (post-adjustments), eliminating the rental losses + paying off debts creates $4,527/mo swing
7. **Concentrated risk**: Single property, poorly maintained, in a softening market — opposite of diversification
8. **Debt reduction guaranteed return**: Paying off 6.99% HELOC is a guaranteed, risk-free 6.99% return
9. **Stagflation risk cuts both ways**: Real estate can be *illiquid* during stagflation — you might not be able to sell when you need to
10. **Stepped-up basis benefit is decades away**: You're 40s — the "hold until death" argument has a very long time horizon

### Bottom Line

**The inflation hedge argument is theoretically valid but practically weak for your specific property.** A good inflation hedge via real estate requires positive cash flow, reasonable cap rates, and a property in a market with strong rent growth. Your rental has none of these. You're paying $4,860/yr to hold an illiquid asset generating sub-1% returns on ~$700K of equity, in a market where prices are falling 16% and inventory is surging 49%.

The rational move is to sell and redeploy: pay off high-interest debt (guaranteed 7% return on HELOC), build an emergency fund, and invest the remainder in a diversified portfolio that actually generates income. Even a conservative 60/40 portfolio would produce $42K–49K/yr vs. your current -$4,860/yr — a $47K–54K annual swing.

The only scenario where holding makes sense is if you believe Seattle Eastside appreciation will exceed 8–10%/yr for the next 5+ years, which contradicts current market data.
