## 2. The Bear Case: Who Predicts a Recession and Why

### A. Iran War / Oil Shock (THE dominant new risk)

**What happened**: The US-Israel war on Iran has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, which carries ~20% of global crude oil and natural gas. Brent crude surged to **$119.50/barrel** on March 9 (up 29%), crossing $100 for the first time since 2022. Goldman Sachs warned oil could reach **$150/barrel** by end of March without resolution.

As of March 10, oil pulled back to $83.45 after unconfirmed reports of Navy escort operations, but the situation remains highly volatile.

**Why it matters for recession**: Higher oil = higher gas prices = higher transportation/food costs = reduced consumer discretionary spending = potential stagflation (rising prices + slowing growth). The Fed is now expected to delay rate cuts until September (was July before the war).

### B. February Jobs Shock

The BLS reported **-92,000 nonfarm payrolls** in February 2026 — a massive miss vs. the +50,000 expected. Unemployment rose to **4.4%** (from 4.3%). December and January were also revised lower by 69,000 combined.

- This is the first negative payroll print in years
- Unemployment has risen from 3.7% lows in 2024 to 4.4% — approaching the **Sahm Rule** trigger (0.50pp rise from 12-month low in the 3-month moving average)
- Job losses concentrated in healthcare (strikes), information technology, financial services, and federal government (DOGE cuts)

### F. David Rosenberg: "Very Significant" Recession in 2027

Rosenberg Research president David Rosenberg sees a serious contraction once two current tailwinds fade:
1. **Fiscal stimulus drying up** — deficit-funded spending has been masking underlying weakness
2. **AI capex peaking** — tech companies will eventually slow massive AI infrastructure investment

He argues the consumer is increasingly **dependent on debt** to sustain spending (a "rupture disguised as resilience"). However, he is contrarian on inflation — expects it to *crater* even as oil rises, as demand destruction takes hold.

## 3. The Bull Case: Who Says No Recession and Why

### A. Goldman Sachs: "Contrarian Optimism"

CEO David Solomon sees strong tailwinds: fiscal stimulus, AI investment, and deregulation. Goldman's baseline GDP forecast was **2.5-2.8%** (the most bullish on Wall Street). However, the firm has since raised recession probability to 35%, showing even the bulls are hedging.

Their biggest concern is a **stock market correction** — economist Pierfrancesco Mei warned a 10% equity drop could shave 0.5pp off GDP growth via the wealth effect.

Sources:
- https://www.thestreet.com/economy/goldman-sachs-delivers-contrarian-take-on-the-economy
- https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/24/biggest-risk-to-the-economy-now-goldman-says-its-a-stock-market-correction.html
- https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/forecasts-for-the-worlds-biggest-economies-in-2026

### B. Ed Yardeni: Structural Bull (Now Hedging)

Yardeni Research has been one of the most vocal bulls, citing:
- **Productivity boom** from AI — GDP growth could reach 3.5-4.5% regime
- Corporate earnings growth of 8-17% expected in 2026
- No structural imbalances in household/corporate balance sheets
- Historical pattern of forecasters repeatedly predicting recessions that don't come

**BUT**: After the Iran war, Yardeni raised his "meltdown" probability from 20% to **35%** and cut his "meltup" scenario from 20% to just **5%**. He still thinks the US will *probably* avoid a downturn, but admits "we can't rule out a bear market and even a recession."

US GDP grew **2.2% in 2025** (despite a government shutdown that shaved ~1pp in Q4). This again surprised forecasters who predicted slowdowns. The shutdown effect is expected to reverse in Q1 2026.

### F. Fed Has Room to Cut

The Fed funds rate is still elevated, giving the Fed significant ammunition to cut rates if the economy weakens. Markets expect the first cut in July-September 2026. EY forecasts 50bps of easing in 2026.

Unlike 2007, household and corporate balance sheets are broadly healthy. Goldman's own research notes that "private-sector balance sheets are healthy across households, corporations, and banks" — the vulnerability is more in private credit and speculative pockets than in the broad economy.

## 5. Historical Comparison

### Current vs. Pre-2007 (Great Financial Crisis)

| Factor | 2006-2007 | 2026 |
|--------|-----------|------|
| **Household debt** | Massively overleveraged (subprime mortgages) | Broadly healthy; debt-to-income ratios lower |
| **Banking system** | Toxic MBS exposure, undercapitalized | Well-capitalized banks; concern is in **private credit** ($1.8T) |
| **Housing market** | Bubble bursting | No comparable bubble |
| **Employment** | Strong before sudden collapse | Weakening but not collapsing |
| **Oil shock** | Oil hit $147 in mid-2008 | Oil hit $119 in March 2026 (Iran war) |
| **Fed policy** | Rate cuts began Sep 2007 | Fed still on hold; cuts expected mid-2026 |
| **Consumer confidence** | Declining | 12-year lows |
| **LEI** | Declining 6+ months before recession | 5 consecutive monthly declines |

**Jamie Dimon explicitly drew this comparison**, warning that "dumb things" in private credit lending echo 2005-2007 behavior. But Goldman notes that unlike 2007, private-sector balance sheets are broadly healthy — the risk is concentrated in shadow lending, not systemic.

### Current vs. 2019 (Pre-COVID)

| Factor | Late 2019 | Early 2026 |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Unemployment** | 3.5% (50-year low) | 4.4% (rising) |
| **GDP growth** | ~2.1% | 2.2% (2025), forecasts 1.9-2.8% for 2026 |
| **Manufacturing PMI** | Contracting (sub-50) | Expanding (52.4) |
| **Consumer confidence** | High | 12-year lows |
| **External shock** | None anticipated (COVID was a surprise) | Active war in Iran + oil shock (known risk) |
| **Yield curve** | Had been inverted | Was inverted, now flat/steepening |
| **Fed stance** | Cutting rates (3 cuts in 2019) | On hold, cuts expected mid-year |
| **Fiscal deficit** | ~$1T | Much larger |

**Key difference**: The 2019 economy was healthy and the recession was caused by an exogenous shock (COVID). In 2026, multiple stressors are building simultaneously — tariffs, DOGE cuts, Iran war/oil shock, AI displacement — but the economy entered 2026 in reasonably strong shape.

## Summary Assessment

**The consensus is NOT a recession in 2026 — but the risks have escalated dramatically in the past two weeks.**

Before the Iran war (early March), Wall Street was broadly optimistic: 2.2-2.8% GDP growth, soft landing intact. The Iran war and February jobs report changed the calculus:

- **Prediction markets**: ~36-40% recession probability (nearly doubled in one week)
- **Wall Street base case**: Still no recession, but every major forecaster has raised risk estimates
- **The swing factor**: Duration of the Iran war and Strait of Hormuz closure. If oil stays above $100 for months, recession becomes the base case. If resolved quickly, the economy likely absorbs the shock.
- **Deloitte's timeline**: Even without a prolonged war, their model shows recession arriving in **2027** as fiscal stimulus and AI capex fade

The economy entered 2026 with solid fundamentals but faces a convergence of risks (Iran war + tariffs + DOGE cuts + AI displacement + consumer confidence collapse) that could tip a slowing economy into contraction. The next 4-8 weeks — particularly the trajectory of oil prices and the March jobs report — will be decisive.
