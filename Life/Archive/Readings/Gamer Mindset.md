I pick up a game, and I try to master it as fast as possible. People have sometimes asked me: "How do you get so good at everything you play?" My answer's always the same: It's just a mindset. One you develop by actually getting really good at something. Once you have it, it sticks with you for anything you do.

### 1. Identify the game you're playing

First and foremost: I identify the game I'm playing. People often think that because they've played similar games, they are supposed to instantly be good at them. Well, that might be true in some cases, but often it's not. Every game has its own strategies, things that make it unique. I ask myself questions like:

- “What is the real purpose of this game?”
- “What's the thing I do that makes me win most games?”
    
These kinds of questions help me identify what I need to improve in order to get better results.

### 2. Identify your weaknesses

At every game I play, there's likely to be things I pick up really fast and do really well by instinct, but others that I simply can not get a hold on, or even if I do, I might not know a way to improve them. I need to identify those as soon as possible, as they are the primary thing that's going to prevent me from improving.

### 3. Copy

I could say this a thousand ways, but it's really as simple as this. There are many ways I do this: watching streams, asking friends, even adding random people sometimes for help in a particular point. Honestly, I've learnt more from adding people to whine about a game and then have an actual nice chat with them than any other factor combined. Usually, they have different points of view than I am accustomed to, and they can teach me things I could have never thought of by myself. This is important.

**Do not be afraid to admit you are not the best.** There are thousands of people who are better than you at any given game. Use that to your advantage. Watch what they do, see how they react to certain situations, and try to understand _why_ they do it. Once you understand the logic behind their actions, you can start implementing it into your own playstyle.
### 4. Practice with Purpose

Most people "just play." They log on, hit the play button, and go through the motions. If you want to master a game, you have to practice with a specific goal in mind.

- Don't just "play League."
- Play a game where your only focus is hitting 80 CS by 10 minutes.
- Play a game where your only goal is to look at the mini-map every 5 seconds.

By breaking the game down into smaller, manageable skills, you can master them one by one until they become second nature.
### 5. Review and Analyze

After a game—especially a loss—I take a moment to reflect. It’s easy to blame teammates or "bad luck," but that doesn't make me a better player. I ask: "What could I have done differently to change the outcome?" Even if a teammate played poorly, there is almost always a moment where a better decision from me could have salvaged the situation.

### 6. Keep a Positive Attitude

The moment you get "tilted" or angry, your ability to learn drops to zero. You start making emotional decisions instead of logical ones. Mastering a game is a marathon, not a sprint. You will lose games. You will make mistakes. The key is to treat every failure as a data point—a lesson that helps you get closer to your goal.

### 7. Consistency is Key

Mastery doesn't happen in a single eight-hour session; it happens through consistent, focused effort over time. It is better to play two games a day with 100% focus than ten games a day while on autopilot. Your brain needs time to process what you’ve learned and turn it into muscle memory.

### 8. The "Ego" Trap

The biggest hurdle for most players is their own ego. If you think you’re already "good enough" or that you’re stuck in "elo hell" because of others, you stop looking for ways to improve yourself. The "Gamer's Mindset" is about constant humility—the belief that you can always be faster, smarter, and more efficient.
