### Explore/Exploit

- Simply put, exploration is *gathering* information, and *exploitation* is using the information you have to get a known good result.
- Never exploring is no way to live. Never exploiting can be every bit as bad.
- A family gathering together on the holidays is **exploitation**. So is a bookworm settling into a reading chair with a hot cut of coffee, or a band playing their greatest hits to a crowd of adoring fans.
- Exploration can be a curse: there are constantly new things (music etc). When you constantly have to explore the new you can never enjoy the fruits of your connoisseurship - a particular kind of hell. 
- In computer science, the tension between exploration and exploitation takes its most concrete form in a scenario called the "multi-armed bandit problem": you walk in a casino with many slot machines. You're interested in maximizing your total winnings. It's clear that this is going to involve some combination of pulling the arms on different machines to test them out (**exploring**), and favoring the most promising machines you've found (**exploiting**).
- Choosing a restaurant or an album is, in effect, a matter of deciding which arm to pull in life's casino. Understanding the explore/exploit tradeoff also provides fundamental insights into how our goals should change as we age, and why the most rational course of action isn't always trying to choose the best.
- So given what you know about two slot machine (6:9 and 1:2 winning ratio), which arms should you pull? It's a trick question, it completely depends on something we haven't discussed yet: how long you plan to be in the casino.
- When balancing favorite experiences and new ones, nothing matters as much as the interval over which we plan to enjoy them. A sobering property of trying new things is that the value of exploration, of finding new favorite, can only go down over time, as the remaining opportunities to savor it dwindle. Discovering an enchanting cafe on your last night in town doesn't give you the opportunity to return.
- The flip side is that the value of exploitation can only go up over time. So explore when you will have time to use the resulting knowledge, exploit when you're ready to cash in. The interval makes the strategy.
- Since the interval makes the strategy, then by observing the strategy we can also infer the interval. For example, from a studio's perspective, a sequel is a movie with a guaranteed fan base: a cash cow, a sure thing, an exploit. The sequels are more likely than brand-new movies to be hits this year, but where will the beloved franchises of the future come from? By entering an almost purely exploit-focused phase, the film industry seems to be signaling a belief that it is near the end of its interval.
- **Win-Stay, lose-switch**: choose an arm at random, and keep pulling it as long as it keeps paying off. If the arm doesn't pay off after a particular pull, then switch to the other one. Way-stay turns out to be an element of the optimal strategy for balancing exploration and exploitation under a wide range of conditions. But lose-shift is another story. Good options shouldn't be penalized too strongly for being impefect. 
- **Gittins Index**:  for every slot machine we know little or nothing about, there is some guaranteed payout rate which, if offered to us in lieu of that machine (like a bribe), will make us quite content never to pull its handle again. This number is Gittins index, suggests an obvious strategy on the casino floor: always play the arm with the highest index.
- The index strategy turned out to be more than a good approximation. It completely solves the multi-armed bandit with geometrically discounted payoffs.  The tension between exploration and exploitation resolves into the simpler task of maximizing a single quantity that accounts for both. 
- Looking at Gittins index, you can see the win-stay principle at work. You can also see where lose-shift would get you into trouble. 
- The most interesting part of the Gittins index table (with 90% discounting) is the top-left entry. An arm that's complete unknown -- has an expected value of 0.5 but a Gittins index of 0.7. In other words, something you have no experience with whatsoever is more attractive than a machine that you know pays out 70% of the time!  With 99% discounting, with the future weighted nearly as heavily as the present, the value of making a chance discovery, relative to taking a sure thing, goes up even more. A totally untested machine is worth a guaranteed 87% chance of a payout!
- The old adage tells us that "the grass is always greener on the other side of the fence". The math tells us why: the unknown has a chance of being better, even if we actually expect it to be no different, or if it's just as liekly to be worse. The untested rookie is worth more (early in the season, ayway) than the veteran of seemingly equal ability, precisely because we know less about him. Exploration in itself has value, since trying new things increases our chances of finding the best. So taking the future into account, rather than focusing just on the present, drives us toward novelty.
- The Gittins index is optimal only under some strong assumptions. It's based on geometric discounting of future reward, valuing each pull at a constant fraction of the previous one, which is something that a variety of experiments in behavioral economics and psychology suggest people don't do. And if there's a cost to switching among options, the Gittins strategy is no longer optimal either. The grass on the other side of the fence doesn't necessarily warrant climbing the fence -- let alone taking out a second mortgage. Even more importantly, it's hard to compute the Gittins index on the fly.
- If Gittins index is too complicated, then you have another option: focus on *regret*.  When we choose what to eat, who to spend time with, or what city to live in, regret looms large -- presented with a set of good options, it is easy to torture ourselves with the consequences of making the wrong choice. These regrets are often about the things we failed to do, the options we never tried. In the memorable words of management theorist Chester Barnard, "To try and fail is at least to learn; to fail to try is to suffer the inestimable loss"
- The success of Upper Confidence Bound algorithms offers a formal justification for the benefit of the doubt. Following the advice of these algorithms, you should be excited to meet new people and try new things - to assume the best about them, in the absense of evidence to the contrary. In the long run, optimism is the best prevention for regret. 
- In general, it seems that people tend to over-explore -- to favor the new disproportionately over the best. 
- Thinking about children as simply being at the transitory exploration stage of a lifelong algorithm might provide some solace for parents of preschoolers. But it also provides new insights about the rationality of children. Pressing buttons at random, being very interested in new toys, and jumping quickly from one thing to another are all things that kids are really great at. And those are exactly what they should be doing if their goal is exploration. A baby putting every object into its mouse is like studiously pulling all the handles at the casino.
- When we talk about decisoin making, we usually focus just on the immediate payoff of a single decision -- and if you treat every decision as if it were your last, then indeed only exploitation makes sense. But over a lifetime, you're going to make a lot of decisions. And it's actually rational to emphasize exploration -- the new rather than the best, the exciting rather than the safe, the random rather than the considered -- for many of those choices, particularly earlier in life.
- At the other extreme from toddlers we have the elderly. Thinking about aging from the perspective of the explore/exploit dilemma also provides some surprising insights into how we should expect our lives to change as time goes on. 
- Laura Carstensen has investigated exactly how, and why, people's social pattern is clear: the size of people's social networks almost invariably decreases over time. She has argued that, in fact, the elderly have fewer social relationship by choice. These decreases are "the result of lifelong selection processes by which people strategically and adaptively cultivate their social networks to maximize social and emotional gains and minimize social and emotional risks." Shrinking of social networks with aging is due primarily to "pruning" perifpheral relationships and focusing attention instead on a core of close friends and family members. Elder people have the same preference if they were able to live 20 years longer. The point is that these differences are not about age -- they are about where people perceive themselves to be on the interval relevent to their decison.

## Sorting
- The unit cost of sorting, instead of falling, rises. Minimizing our pain and suffering when it comes to sorting is all about minimizing the number of things we have to sort.
- Breaking the quadratic barrier: divide and conquer
- We humans sort more than our data, more than our possessions. We sort ourselves.
- The World Cap, the Olympics, the NCAA, NFL, NHL, NBA, and MLB --- all of these implicitly implement sorting procedures. Their seasons, ladders, and playoffs are algorithms for producing rank order.
- One of the most familiar algorithms in sports is the Round-Robin format, where each of n teams eventually plays every one of the other n-1 teams.  It's the most laborious. Ladder tournaments -- popular in sports like badminton, squash, and racquetball -- put players in a linear ranking, which each player allowed to issue a direct challenge to the player immediately above them, exchanging places if they prevail. This is bubble sort. The most prevalent tournament format, is a bracket tournament -- as in the famous NCAA basketball "March Madness": similar to a mergesort but shortened: eliminated teams are left unsorted.
- Sports are not always designed strictly to minimize the number of games. For example, baseball's regular season of 2430 games. Why do they do n^2 in order to just get the top, if that's all they care about, if we know we can do a full sort in linearithmic time. Minimizing the number of games isn't actually in the league's interest.
- Soccer's low scores make game outcomes much closer to random than most fans would perfer to imagine. A 3:2 score gives the winning team only a 5-in-8 chance of actually being a better team. Even a 6:1 blowout leaves a 7% chance that it was a statistical fluke. Computer scientists call this phenomenon noise.
- Inefficient bubble sort makes it fairly robust against noise, compared to Mergesort in which each comparison potentially moves an item a long way. The single best algorithm in the face of a noisy comparator is Comparison Counting Sort. 
- What if head-to-head comparisons happened only voluntarily? A substantial portion of poker is still played in what are known as "cash games," where two or more players spontaneously agree to play with real money on the line with every hand.
- Displacement happens when an animal uses its knowledge of the hierarchy to determine that a particular confrontation simply isn't worth it. In many animal societies, resources and opportunities -- food, mates, preferred spaces, and so forth -- are scarce, and somehow it must be decided who gets what. Establishing an order ahead of time is less violent than coming to blows every time a mating opportunitity or a prime spot of grass becomes available. Bilologist tend to think of pecking orders as the violence that preempts violence. Sounds familiar? It's the search-sort tradeoff.
- Animal behavior implies that the number of hostile confrontations encountered by each individual will grow substantially, at least logarithmically, and perhaps quadratically, as the group gets bigger. Aggression appears to go away after a period of some weeks, unless new members are added to the flock, corroborating the idea that the group is sorting itself.
- The key to thinking about decentralized sorting in nature, is that dominance hierarchies are ultimately information hierachies. It comes down to how good the protagonists are at keeping track of the current order.
	- I'm one of the top heads-up, no-limit hold'em players in the world, and in my head I have a fairly specific ranking of who I think the twenty or so best players are, and I think each of them has a similar ranking in their mind. I think there is a pretty high degree of consensus about what the list looks like. Only when these rankings differ will cash games ensure.
- There are ways of making order without costs: a race instead of a fight.
- The Fortune 500 list, to the extent that it creates a kidn of corporate hierarchy, is one of these. Having a benchmark -- any benchmark -- solves the computational problem of scaling up a sort.
- By and large, any pair of people can tell, without needing to negotiate, who is supposed to show what level of respect to whom. 
- When we think about the factors that make large-scale human societies possible, it's easy to focus on technologies. But the cultural practice of measuring status with quantifiable metrics might be just as important. E.g. respect your elders. The same principle is at work between nations as within them. It is often noted that a benchmark like national GDP -- which underlies the invite lists to diplomatic summits such as the G20 -- is a crude, imperfect measurement. But the existence of any benchmark at all transforms the question of national status from one demanding at least a linearithmic number of tussles and resolutions into something with a single reference point that ranks all
- Much as we bemoan the daily rat race, the fact that it's a race rather than a fight is a key part of what sets us apart from the monkeys, the chickens -- and, for that matter, the rats.

### Caching

- Caching plays a critical role in the architecture of memory, and it underlies everything from the layout of processor chips at the millimeter scale to the geography of the global Internet. It offers a new perspective on all the various storage systems and memory banks of human life -- not only our machines, but also our closets, our offices, our libraries. And our heads.
- Belady compared Random Eviction, FIFO, and LRU and found LRU consistently performed the closest to clairvoyance. The LRU principle is effective because of something computer scientists call "temporal locality." It emerges in the way people solve problems too. LRU teaches us that the next thing we can expect to need is the last one we needed, while the thing we'll need after that is probably the second-most-recent one. And the last thing we can expect to need is the one we've already gone longest without.
- Libraries are a natural example of a memory hierarchy when used in concert with our own desk space. In fact, libraries in themselves, with their various sections and storage facilities, are a great example of a memory hierarchy with multiple levels. As a consequence, they face all sorts of caching problems.
- CDN/Alkima is the cache for internet, based on the insight that in-demand files should be stored near the location where they are used (instead of different memory media), also translates into purely physical environment. For example, Amazon's enormous fulfillment centers. Employees are told to place incoming items wherever they can find space in the warehouse and tag their location.
- At home, when you are deciding what to keep and what to throw away, LRU is potentially a good principle to use -- much better than FIFO. Also exploit geography. Make sure things are in whatever cache is closest to the place where they're typically used. For example, a doctor put the vacuum cleaner bags behind the couch in the living room, because that's where it is most likely needed.
- Having multiple levels of caches -- from smallest and fastest to largest and slowest -- can be even better. Where your belongings are concerned, your closet is one cache level, your basement another, and a self storage locker a third. 
- Tom's otherwise extremely tolerant wife objects to a pile of closes next to the bed, despite his insistence that it's in fact a highly efficient caching scheme.
- The mathematics of self-organizing lists suggests something radical: the big pile of papers on your desk, far from being a guilt-inducing fester of chaoes, is actually one of the most well-designed and efficient structures available. Tossing things back on the top of the pile is the very best you can do, shy of knowing the future.
- The key idea behind Anderson's new account of human memory is that the problem might be not one of storage, but of organization. The mind has essentially infinite capacity for memories, but we have only a finite amount of time in which to search for them. Anderson made the analogy to a library with a single, arbitrarily long shelf. The key to a good human memory then becomes the same as the key to a good computer cache: predicting which items are most likely to be wanted in the future.
- If the pattern by which things fade from our minds is the very pattern by which things fade from use around us, then there may be a very good explanation indeed for the Ebbinghaus forgeting curve -- namely, that it's a perfect tuning of the brain to the world, making available precisely the things most likely to be needed.
- If these tradeoffs (too expensive to maintain access to an unbounded number of items for the brain) really are unavaidable, and the brain appears to be optimally tuned to the world around it, then what we refer to as the inevitable "cognitive decline" that comes with age may in fact be something else.
- Size alone is enough to impair speed. Larger city is slower to get around, larger library is slower to navigate. Level 1 cache is small in size for CPU because the size needs to be small.
- If the fundamental challenge of memory really is one of organization rather than storage, perhaps it should change how we think about the impact of aging on our mental abilities.  Recent work has suggested that what we call "cognitive decline" -- lags and retrieval errors -- may not be about the search process slowing or deteriorating, but (at least partly) an unavoidable consequence of the amount of information we have to navigate getting bigger and bigger. Older brains -- which must manage a greater store of memories -- are literally solving harder computational problems with every passing day. This impact of extra information on human memory is demonstrated by language. Simply knowing more makes things harder when it comes to recognizing words, names, and even letters. No matter how good your organization scheme is, having to search through more things will inevitably take longer. It's not that we're forgetting; it's that we're remembering. We're becoming archives.
- I think the most important tangible thing seniors can do is to try to get a handle on the idea that their minds are natural information processing devices. Some things that might seem frustrating as we grow older are a function of the amount of stuff we have to sift through and are not necessarily a sign of a failing mind. A lot of what is currently called decline is simply learning.


## Scheduling
- Earliest Due Date strategy: start with the task due soonest and work your way towards the task due last.
	- How long each task will take to complete is entirely irrelevant.
	- It assumes you are only interested in one metric: reducing your maximum lateness. 
	- Bad scenario: produce waiting to be put into refrigerator: it will minimize the rottenness of the most rotten thing you'll have to eat, which is not what we want.
- Moore's Algorithm: minimize the number of foods that spoil
	- We start out just like with Earliest Due Date, by scheduling out our produce in order of spoilage date, earliest first, one item at a time. However, as soon as it looks like we won't get to eating the next item in time, we pause, look back over the meals we already planned, and throw out the biggest item (the one that will take the most days to consume, e.g. watermelon that would take a half dozen servings to eat). Repeat until everything remains can be eaten in order of spoilage date without anything spoiling.
	- Moore's algorithm minimizes the number of items you'll need to throw away.
- Shortest Processing Time: always do the quickest task you can.
	- Take the outsider's perspective. Two tasks which takes 1d and 4d. If you do the 4d first and 1d task later, the customer has waited a total of 4+5=9 days. If you do the 1d first and 4d later, the customer has waited a total of 1+5=6 days.
	- Shortest Processing Time get things done. The book `Getting things done` recommends immediately perform any task that takes less than 2 minutes. It will ease your mind by shrinking the number of outstanding tasks as quickly as possible.
	- What if there is a "weight" (importance/priority) for each task? The optimal strategy is devide the weight of each task by how long it will take to finish, and then work in order from the highest resulting importance-per-unit-time (like "density") to the lowest.
	- In business, it can easily translates to devide project size (or total bill) by duration (which becomes the daily rate or hourly rate) as the scheduling metric.
	- Also applies to debt reduction strategy: pay off the debt with the highest interest rate first.
- **Priority Inversion**: a low-priority task takes possession of a system resource (access to a database for example) to do some work, but is then interrupted partway through that work by a timer, which pauses it and invokes the system scheduler. The scheduler tees up a high-priority task, but it can't run because the database is occupied. And so the scheduler moves down the priority list, running various unblocked medium-priority tasks instead - rather than the high-priority one (which is blocked), or the low-priority one that's blocking it (which is stuck in line behind all the medium-priority work).
	- Pathfinder on Mars bug.
	- Solution: Priority inheritance. If a low-priority task is found to be blocking a high-priority resource, well, then all of a sudden that low-priority task should become the highest-priority thing on the system, "inheriting" the priority of the thing it's blocking.
	- The moral here is that a love of getting things done isn't enough to avoid scheduling pitfalls, and neither is a love of getting important things done. The very desire to make immediate progress is how one gets stuck.
- **Precedence Constraint**: a certain task can't be started until another one is finished.
	- A textbook case of priority inversion caused by precedence constraints.
- Drop Everything: Preemption and Uncertainty
	- Makes scheduling easier.
	- When a task's starting time comes, compare that task to the one currently under way. If you're working by Earliest Due Date and the new task is due even sooner than the current one, switch gears; otherwise stay the course. Likewise, if you're working by Shortest Processing Time, and the new task can be finished faster than the current one, pause to take care of it first; otherwise, continue with what you were doing.
	- It turns out that even if you don't know when tasks will begin, Earliest Due Date and Shortest Processing Time are still optimal strategies, able to guarantee you (on average) the best possible performance in the face of uncertainty. If assignments get tossed on your desk at unpredictable moments, the optimal strategy of minimizing maximum lateness is still the preemptive version of Earliest Due Date.  Similarly, the preemptive version of Shortest Processing Time, is still optimal for minimizing the sum of completition times.
	- In fact, the weighted version of Shortest Processing Time is a pretty good candidate for best general-purpose scheduling strategy in the face of uncertainty. It offers a simple prescription for time management: each time a new piece of work come in, divide its importance by the amount of time it will take to complete. If that figure is higher than for the task you're currently doing, switch to the new one.
