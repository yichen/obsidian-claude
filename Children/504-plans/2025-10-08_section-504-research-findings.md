## Federal Section 504 Requirements

### What is Section 504?
Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 is a federal civil rights law that:
- Prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in programs receiving federal financial assistance
- Requires schools to provide a **Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)** to students with disabilities
- Ensures students cannot be excluded from participation or denied benefits solely due to disability

### Key Protections:
- **Program accessibility** - Schools must ensure programs are accessible to students with disabilities
- **Reasonable accommodations** - Schools must provide modifications to policies, practices, and procedures
- **Non-discriminatory discipline** - Schools must make reasonable modifications to disciplinary policies for students with disabilities
- **Effective communication** - Schools must provide effective communication for students with hearing or vision disabilities

### Enforcement
- Enforced by the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR)
- Each federal agency has its own Section 504 regulations
- Parents can file complaints with OCR if rights are violated

## Washington State Context

Section 504 is a federal law that applies uniformly across all states, including Washington. Key points:

- Washington public schools must comply with federal Section 504 requirements
- The law aims to ensure students with disabilities are not discriminatorily disciplined
- Schools must proactively identify and meet behavioral, social, emotional, and academic needs of students with disabilities
- Schools should prevent behaviors that might result in discipline through proper support

## How to Work with Issaquah School District on 504 Plans

### 1. Request an Evaluation
- **Put your request in writing** - Email or written letter to school principal or 504 coordinator
- **Describe your child's disability** and how it affects their learning
- **Request a 504 evaluation** explicitly
- Keep a copy of your request

### 2. Gather Documentation
- **Medical documentation** of your child's disability
- **Functional impact** - Document how the disability impacts major life activities (learning, reading, concentrating, communicating, etc.)
- **Examples** - Specific instances where your child has struggled due to their disability
- **Keep copies** - Maintain copies of all correspondence and documentation

### 3. The 504 Evaluation Process
- School will evaluate whether your child qualifies under Section 504
- A child qualifies if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities
- Schools may use existing data, observations, and assessments
- Parents have the right to provide input during evaluation

### 4. The 504 Team Meeting
- **Participate actively** - You are a critical member of the 504 team
- **Bring documentation** - Medical records, examples, teacher observations
- **Be specific** about accommodations you believe would help your child
- **Ask questions** - Make sure you understand all proposed accommodations
- **Take notes** - Document what is discussed and agreed upon

### 5. The 504 Plan
A 504 plan should include:
- Description of the disability
- How the disability affects major life activities
- Specific accommodations and modifications
- Who is responsible for implementing each accommodation
- Review date

Common accommodations may include:
- Extended time on tests
- Preferential seating
- Modified assignments
- Behavioral support plans
- Assistive technology
- Breaks during class

### 6. Your Rights as a Parent
- **Right to participate** in decisions about your child's education
- **Right to examine records** related to your child
- **Right to an impartial hearing** if you disagree with the school's decisions
- **Right to file complaints** with the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights
- **Right to request changes** to the 504 plan at any time

### 7. Implementation and Follow-Up
- **Communicate regularly** with teachers about implementation
- **Monitor effectiveness** - Is the plan working? Are accommodations being provided?
- **Request modifications** if accommodations aren't working
- **Annual review** - Plans should be reviewed at least annually (request more frequent reviews if needed)
- **Document issues** - Keep records of any problems with implementation

### 8. If You Disagree with the School
- **Request a meeting** to discuss concerns
- **Put concerns in writing** - Email or letter to 504 coordinator
- **Request an impartial hearing** - You have the right to due process
- **File a complaint with OCR** - If you believe the school is violating Section 504
- **Seek advocacy support** - Contact PAVE or Arc of Washington State

## Best Practices for Parents

### Communication Tips
1. **Keep everything in writing** - Follow up verbal conversations with email summaries
2. **Be specific** - Clearly describe your child's needs and requested accommodations
3. **Stay collaborative** - Work with the school as a team when possible
4. **Be persistent** - Don't be afraid to advocate firmly for your child
5. **Document everything** - Keep a binder with all 504-related documents

### Preparation Tips
1. **Research** - Understand your child's disability and effective accommodations
2. **Network** - Connect with other parents of children with similar needs
3. **Educate yourself** on Section 504 rights and procedures
4. **Bring support** - Consider bringing an advocate or support person to meetings
5. **Stay organized** - Keep all documents, emails, and notes in one place

### Red Flags to Watch For
- School delays evaluation without valid reason
- School refuses to provide requested accommodations without explanation
- Accommodations in the plan are not being implemented
- School makes changes to the plan without parent input
- School suggests dropping the 504 plan without proper review

### When to Seek Legal Help
Consider consulting an education attorney or advocate if:
- The school refuses to evaluate your child
- The school denies a 504 plan without valid reason
- Accommodations are repeatedly not implemented
- Your child is being disciplined for disability-related behavior
- You've filed complaints but issues persist
